Part 1 2024 Tax Year

Your 2024 return was filed with employment income of $44,096.30, net income of $43,690.34, and a refund of $3,903.65. The CRA has reassessed the return and is asking for approximately $2,200 more.

1.1 Why the CRA Reassessment ($2,200)

You claimed the Home Buyers' Amount of $10,000 on line 31270 of your return. This provides a 15% non-refundable credit worth $1,500 federally, plus a corresponding Ontario credit. The CRA has likely disallowed this because the Home Buyers' Amount requires that you intend to occupy the home as your principal place of residence within one year of purchase.

Since you purchased the property as a rental investment and never lived in it, you do not qualify. The CRA can verify this from your ON-BEN form, which shows you were paying rent at other addresses throughout 2024.

1.2 Can You Claim Rental Expenses for 2024?

You were renovating the property from May to approximately February/March 2025. During this renovation period, the CRA has specific "soft cost" rules under ITA subsection 18(3.1) that govern how carrying costs are treated.

Soft costs are carrying expenses incurred during a construction or renovation period: mortgage interest, property taxes, insurance, utilities, and legal/accounting fees.

The key rule: Soft costs during a renovation period can only be deducted as current expenses up to the amount of rental income earned. Since you had $0 rental income in 2024, you cannot deduct soft costs as current expenses to create a rental loss. They must be added to the capital cost of the building.

2024 Soft Cost (May–Dec)EstimatedTreatment
Mortgage interest (8 months)~$16,500Add to building capital cost
Property taxes (8 months)~$2,600–$4,000Add to building capital cost
Insurance (8 months)~$800–$1,300Add to building capital cost
Utilities (8 months)~$1,000–$2,000Add to building capital cost
Total soft costs~$21,000–$24,000Added to ACB / CCA pool

The actual renovation expenditures (materials, contractor labour, permits) are capital expenses added to the building's capital cost (Class 1 at 4% CCA) or to the appropriate CCA class for specific items (e.g., Class 8 at 20% for appliances).

Future Benefit
While these costs don't give you a tax break in 2024, they increase your building's cost base. This means: (1) a larger CCA pool to draw from in profitable years, and (2) a lower capital gain when you eventually sell. On ~$21,000 of soft costs alone, this could save you $4,000–$4,400 in capital gains tax on sale.
1.3 Should You Amend Your 2024 Return?

Yes. Even though you cannot deduct the soft costs as current expenses, you should file a T1 adjustment (T1-ADJ) for 2024 to:

  1. Add a T776 Statement of Real Estate Rentals to properly record the rental property
  2. Set up the CCA schedule with the building's cost plus renovation costs plus capitalized soft costs
  3. Establish the rental operation start date for CRA records

This will not change your 2024 tax owing, but it creates a clean foundation for your 2025 deductions.

2.6 CCA Decision
CCA Recommendation
For a single rental property, most tax advisors recommend NOT claiming CCA. When you sell, all previously claimed CCA is "recaptured" and taxed as ordinary income at your full marginal rate. Avoid CCA unless a CPA advises otherwise. For 2025, you'll likely have a loss already, making CCA irrelevant.
2.7 Capital Gains When You Sell (Future)

Since you never lived in the property, you cannot claim the Principal Residence Exemption. When you sell, 50% of the capital gain is taxable (the proposed increase to 66.67% was cancelled by PM Carney in March 2025).

Your Adjusted Cost Base will include: purchase price + land transfer tax + legal fees on purchase + renovation costs + capitalized soft costs. The higher this number, the lower your capital gain on sale.

2.8 GST/HST
  • Resale home: No HST was payable on purchase. No rebate available.
  • New construction: If the property was newly built, you may be eligible for the NRRP Rebate (up to ~$24,000 Ontario). Deadline: 2 years from closing — act before May 2026.
  • Rental income: Long-term residential rent is exempt from HST. Do not charge your tenants HST.
